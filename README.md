# Research-Based Practices for Young Learners in Computer Science and Programming

The development of technical proficiency in computer science and programming for young learners (K-8) is best supported by pedagogical approaches grounded in educational research. This compilation synthesizes 20 best practices drawn from leading educational frameworks and academic studies, focusing on creating an engaging, developmentally appropriate, and rigorous learning environment [1] [2] [3].

## 20 Best Research-Based Practices

The practices are organized into three core themes: **Pedagogical Approach**, **Computational Thinking Skills**, and **Learning Environment**.

### I. Pedagogical Approach (Focus on *How* to Teach)

These practices emphasize the instructional methods and overall teaching philosophy.

| ID | Practice | Core Principle | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | **Lead with Concepts** | Focus on key concepts, terms, and vocabulary to build a shared and consistent understanding before diving into syntax. | [2] |
| 2 | **Unplug, Unpack, Repack** | Introduce complex ideas through familiar, "unplugged" (non-computer) activities, then connect this understanding back to the formal computing concept (Semantic Waves). | [2] |
| 3 | **Model Everything** | Use techniques like live coding and worked examples to explicitly demonstrate processes, from writing code to debugging, providing scaffolding that can be gradually removed. | [2] |
| 4 | **Foster Program Comprehension** | Prioritize activities that consolidate knowledge of program function and structure, such as code tracing, debugging, and Parsons' Problems (rearranging blocks of code). | [2] |
| 5 | **Teach Code Reading/Decoding** | Use strategies like TIPP&SEE (Title, Intention, Parts, Plan, See, Examine, Evaluate) to help students methodically observe and understand existing code before attempting to write their own. | [3] |
| 6 | **Coding as a New Language** | Treat programming as a symbolic system of representation, integrating it with literacy instruction to leverage the critical foundational role of language in early years. | [1] |
| 7 | **Focus on Expressive and Communicative Functions** | Emphasize that coding is a tool for personal expression, storytelling, and communication, rather than solely a technical skill. | [1] |

### II. Computational Thinking Skills (Focus on *What* to Teach)

These practices are rooted in the core components of computational thinking, which is essential for technical proficiency.

| ID | Practice | Core Principle | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 8 | **Algorithmic Thinking** | Explicitly teach students to develop and follow step-by-step procedures (algorithms) to solve problems, both on and off the computer. | [4] |
| 9 | **Decomposition** | Teach students to break down large, complex problems into smaller, more manageable sub-problems. | [4] |
| 10 | **Pattern Recognition** | Train students to observe and identify recurring patterns in data or problems to develop efficient and generalized solutions. | [4] |
| 11 | **Developing and Using Abstractions** | Teach students to manage complexity by focusing on essential information and creating generalized models or rules. | [4] |
| 12 | **Data Collection and Analysis** | Incorporate activities where students collect, manage, and analyze data to inform their problem-solving and programming efforts. | [4] |
| 13 | **Recognizing and Defining Computational Problems** | Guide students to identify real-world problems that can be solved computationally and define the necessary inputs, processes, and outputs. | [4] |
| 14 | **Creating Computational Artifacts** | Encourage students to build tangible or digital products (e.g., programs, models, simulations) to express their ideas and demonstrate their understanding. | [4] |

### III. Learning Environment and Culture (Focus on *How* Students Learn)

These practices focus on the social, emotional, and structural aspects of the learning environment.

| ID | Practice | Core Principle | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 15 | **Cultivate a Growth Mindset** | Create an environment where failure (debugging) is normalized and viewed as a learning opportunity, emphasizing and rewarding the process of perseverance over the result. | [3] |
| 16 | **Work Together (Pair Programming)** | Encourage collaboration, specifically using pair programming and peer instruction, to stimulate dialogue and shared understanding. | [2] |
| 17 | **Create Real-World Relevance** | Use meaningful, real-world problems connected to student values and interests to increase motivation and persistence in STEM. | [3] |
| 18 | **Use Developmentally Appropriate Tools** | Utilize tools like ScratchJr, block-based languages, and physical robots (e.g., KIBO) that are age-appropriate and support play-based, hands-on learning. | [1] |
| 19 | **Integrate with Other Disciplines** | Embed computer science concepts within traditional STEM disciplines (Science, Technology, Engineering, Math) and other subjects to show its broad applicability. | [1] |
| 20 | **Promote Collaboration and Sharing** | Implement gallery walks, presentations, and peer reviews to allow students to see multiple solutions, celebrate their work, and normalize the debugging process. | [3] |

The following resources provide the research foundation and practical examples for these best practices.

### PDF Research Papers and Academic Articles

*   **[1] Coding as another language: A pedagogical approach for teaching computer science in early childhood**
    *   **Author:** Marina Umaschi Bers (2019)
    *   **Source:** *Journal of Computer Education*
    *   **Link:** https://sites.bc.edu/devtech/wp-content/uploads/sites/181/2019/11/Bers2019_Article_CodingAsAnotherLanguageAPedago.pdf
    *   **Relevance:** Details the "Coding as Another Language" (CAL) approach, which treats coding as a new literacy, supporting practices 6, 7, 18, and 19.

*   **[2] How we teach computing: 12 pedagogy principles**
    *   **Source:** National Centre for Computing Education (Raspberry Pi Foundation)
    *   **Link:** https://static.teachcomputing.org/pedagogy/Pedagogy-principles.pdf
    *   **Relevance:** Provides a comprehensive framework for teaching computing, supporting practices 1, 2, 3, 4, and 16.

*   **[3] 7 Research-Based Classroom Strategies for Teaching Computer Science (CS)**
    *   **Source:** Museum of Science (MOS) EiE Team
    *   **Link:** https://yesblog.mos.org/7-research-based-classroom-strategies-for-teaching-computer-science-cs
    *   **Relevance:** Offers practical classroom strategies, supporting practices 5, 15, 17, and 20.
